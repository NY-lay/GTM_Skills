## Principles of Composition

### Use the active voice
Active is more direct and vigorous than passive. Passive buries the actor and weakens the sentence.

| Weak (passive) | Strong (active) |
|---|---|
| My first visit to Boston will always be remembered by me. | I shall always remember my first visit to Boston. |
| A survey of this region was made in 1900. | This region was surveyed in 1900. |
| There were a great number of dead leaves lying on the ground. | Dead leaves covered the ground. |
| The reason that he left college was that his health became impaired. | Failing health compelled him to leave college. |

Avoid passive constructions with *there is / there are / could be heard*. Replace them with a verb.

### Put statements in positive form
Make definite assertions. *Not* is a means of denial — not evasion. Readers want to be told what *is*, not only what *isn't*.

| Weak (negative) | Strong (positive) |
|---|---|
| He was not very often on time. | He usually came late. |
| He did not think that studying Latin was much use. | He thought the study of Latin useless. |
| not honest | dishonest |
| did not remember | forgot |
| did not pay any attention to | ignored |

### Use definite, specific, concrete language
Prefer specific to general, definite to vague, concrete to abstract. The surest way to hold a reader's attention is to be specific.

| Vague | Concrete |
|---|---|
| A period of unfavorable weather set in. | It rained every day for a week. |
| He showed satisfaction as he took possession of his reward. | He grinned as he pocketed the coin. |

Never let go of the concrete, even when dealing with general principles — always give specific instances.

### Omit needless words
Vigorous writing is concise. Every word should earn its place.

**Cut these constructions:**

| Wordy | Concise |
|---|---|
| the question as to whether | whether |
| there is no doubt but that | no doubt / doubtless |
| he is a man who | he |
| in a hasty manner | hastily |
| this is a subject which | this subject |
| owing to the fact that | since / because |
| in spite of the fact that | though / although |
| the fact that he had not succeeded | his failure |

**Cut *who is*, *which was*, and similar when superfluous:**
- "His brother, who is a member of the same firm" → "His brother, a member of the same firm"

**Combine related sentences into one:**
Before: *Macbeth was very ambitious. This led him to wish to become king of Scotland.* (and so on — 51 words)
After: *Encouraged by his wife, Macbeth achieved his ambition... by murdering Duncan and becoming king of Scotland.* (26 words)

### Avoid a succession of loose sentences
A string of clauses joined by *and, but, so, who, which* becomes monotonous. Vary structure: use simple sentences, semicolons, periodic sentences. Don't let a paragraph run on one pattern.

### Express coordinate ideas in similar form (parallel construction)
Similar content and function should look similar. Readers recognize likeness of content when structure matches.

| Broken parallel | Parallel |
|---|---|
| It was both a long ceremony and very tedious. | The ceremony was both long and tedious. |
| A time not for words, but action. | A time not for words, but for action. |

Correlatives (*both/and; not/but; not only/but also; either/or*) must be followed by the same grammatical construction.

### Keep related words together
Subject and verb should not be separated by a clause that can move to the front. Modifiers go next to what they modify.

| Separated | Together |
|---|---|
| He only found two mistakes. | He found only two mistakes. |
| Wordsworth, in the fifth book of The Excursion, gives a description. | In the fifth book of The Excursion, Wordsworth gives a description. |

### Place emphatic words at the end
The end of a sentence is its strongest position. Put the word or phrase you want to land hardest there.

| Weak ending | Strong ending |
|---|---|
| Humanity has hardly advanced in fortitude since that time, though it has advanced in many other ways. | Humanity, since that time, has advanced in many other ways, but it has hardly advanced in fortitude. |

The beginning of a sentence is the second-strongest position. Both are prominent; the middle is weak.

## Rules of Usage (Sentence Clarity)

**Don't join independent clauses with a comma.** Use a semicolon or a period.
- Wrong: *It is nearly half past five, we cannot reach town before dark.*
- Right: *It is nearly half past five; we cannot reach town before dark.*

**Put a comma before a conjunction introducing a coordinate clause.**
- *The situation is perilous, but there is still one chance of escape.*

**Use a comma to enclose parenthetic expressions.**
- *The best way to see a country, unless you are pressed for time, is to travel on foot.*

**A participial phrase at the start must refer to the grammatical subject.**
- Wrong: *Being in a dilapidated condition, I was able to buy the house very cheap.* (house is in dilapidated condition, not I)

## Words and Expressions to Avoid

**Hackneyed, vague, or redundant:**

| Word/phrase | Problem | Use instead |
|---|---|---|
| *factor* | Hackneyed; almost always replaceable | "He won by being better trained" not "training was a key factor" |
| *feature* | Same problem as *factor* | State what the thing actually is or does |
| *character* | Redundant | "hostile acts" not "acts of a hostile character" |
| *nature* | Redundant | "hostile acts" not "acts of a hostile nature" |
| *case* | Usually unnecessary | "Many rooms were poorly ventilated" not "In many cases, the rooms..." |
| *certainly* | Lazy intensifier | Cut it, or use a stronger word |
| *very* | Weak intensifier | Use words strong in themselves; use sparingly |
| *so* | Avoid as intensifier | Not "so good" or "so warm" in writing |
| *interesting* | Don't announce interest — create it | Tell the story; don't say "an interesting story is told" |
| *claim* | Don't use for *say* or *declare* | Reserve for legal/ownership sense |
| *state* | Don't use for *say* or *remark* | Reserve for "express fully or clearly" |
| *dependable* | Needless substitute | Use *reliable* or *trustworthy* |
| *line, along these lines* | Overworked | "He spoke to the same effect" |
| *literal/literally* | Misused for exaggeration | "A flood of abuse" not "a literal flood" |
| *respective/respectively* | Usually omittable | "Listed under the names of their authors" |
| *one of the most* | Threadbare opener | Find a more specific way in |
| *viewpoint* | Imprecise | Use *point of view* or just *view* |
| *worth while* (as adjective) | Indefensible | "Worth reading" not "a worth while story" |
| *while* (for *and* or *but*) | Ambiguous | Use semicolon, or the right conjunction |
| *however* (sentence opener) | Wrong usage | Place *however* mid-sentence; "nevertheless" if first |
| *less* for *fewer* | Misuse | *Less* = quantity; *fewer* = number |
| *like* for *as* | Misuse | *Like* governs nouns; *as* governs clauses |
| *most* for *almost* | Misuse | "Almost everybody" not "most everybody" |

**Grammar:**
- *Data* is plural. *These data were tabulated.*
- *Different from*, not *different than*.
- *Can* = able to. Don't use as substitute for *may*.
